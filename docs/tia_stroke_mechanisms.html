TIA Risk Stratification and Stroke Mechanisms

1 TIA Risk Stratification and Stroke Mechanisms

TIAs:

As a general rule of thumb, moderate/high-risk TIA patients should be observed for 24 hours and evaluated expeditiously for high-risk stroke mechanism. If they want to go home, tell them the risk:

The ABCD2 score predicts risk of stroke within 2 days of a TIA (Lancet 369: 282-292, 2007):

Age ≥60 years [1 point]

Blood pressure ≥140/90 mm Hg [1 point]

Clinical features: unilateral weakness [2 points], speech impairment without weakness [1 point]

Duration ≥60 min [2 points] or 10–59 min [1 point]

Diabetes [1 point]

Score 6–7 = High risk = 8.1% 2-day risk

4–5 = Moderate risk = 4.1%

0–3 = Low risk = 1.0%

Patients with TIA or minor stroke symptoms (non-disabling, NIHSS ≤ 3) can be admitted to the Clinical Decision Unit (CDU) – must be discussed with stroke attending and ED

Stroke mechanisms:

Most common mechanisms of stroke include:

  • Cardioembolism (~20% ischemic stroke): Atrial fibrillation (most common, esp in older adults), CHF/CM with low EF, LV or LAA thrombus, cardiac tumor (myxoma/fibroelastoma), mechanical valve, endocarditis (infectious or marantic [autoimmune/malignancy]), paradoxical embolism (PFO, pulm AVM)

  • Large-artery atherosclerosis (~15%) – extracranial or intracranial (affects treatment choice)

  • Small-vessel disease (~25%) – must be subcortical/brainstem/cerebellum. Small cortical strokes are generally embolic. Lacunar strokes are usually linked to uncontrolled vascular risk factors.

  • Rare causes (<5%) – see below, also consider hypercoagulable state from undiagnosed malignancy

  • Cryptogenic stroke (~20-30%) – no cause found despite thorough workup. If embolic-appearing, may be ESUS (embolic stroke of undetermined source), consider long-term Afib monitoring, no role for empiric AC per ESUS trials to date.

Stroke in Young:

In addition to conventional stroke mechanisms, consider the following for patients <60 years old:

Cardiac disease (including congenital, rheumatic valve disease, mitral valve prolapse, patent foramen ovale, endocarditis, atrial myxoma, arrhythmias, cardiac surgery)

Large vessel disease

  • Premature atherosclerosis – consider radiation vasculopathy in patients with previous head/neck irradiation. Also consider HIV

  • Dissection (spontaneous or traumatic)

  • Inherited metabolic diseases (homocystinuria, Fabry’s, pseudoxanthoma elasticum, MELAS syndrome)

  • Fibromuscular dysplasia

  • Infection (bacterial, fungal, tuberculosis, syphilis, Lyme)

  • Vasculitis (collagen vascular diseases — systemic lupus erythematosus, rheumatoid arthritis, Sjögren’s syndrome, polyarteritis nodosa; Takayasu’s disease, Wegener’s syndrome, cryoglobulinemia, sarcoidosis, inflammatory bowel disease, isolated central nervous system angiitis)

  • Moyamoya disease

  • Toxic (illicit drugs — cocaine, heroin, phencyclidine; therapeutic drugs — L-asparaginase, cytosine arabinoside)

Small vessel disease

  • Vasculopathy (infectious, noninfectious, microangiopathy)

Hematologic disease

  • Sickle-cell disease, leukemia, thrombocytosis, polycythemia vera

  • Hypercoagulable states (antiphospholipid antibody syndromes, deficiency of antithrombin III or protein S or C, resistance to activated protein C, increased factor VIII, MTHFR)

  • DIC, TTP

  • Venous occlusion (dehydration, parameningeal infection, meningitis, neoplasm, polycythemia, leukemia, inflammatory bowel disease)
